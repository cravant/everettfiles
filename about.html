About the Agency

Organizational Brief — Updated February 2026

Historical Overview

The Joseph Coolidge Agency was established on September 18, 1947, under Executive Order 9877, six days after the National Security Act restructured America's intelligence apparatus. Named for Joseph Randolph Coolidge III, a decorated OSS operative who disappeared during Operation PAPERCLIP, the agency was conceived to address a critical gap: threats that fell outside the jurisdiction of traditional law enforcement but required domestic investigative capabilities beyond the scope of foreign intelligence services.

From its inception, JCA operated in the shadows of the early Cold War. While the FBI pursued criminal enterprises and the CIA focused on overseas operations, JCA carved out a unique mandate: counterintelligence operations within U.S. borders, investigation of federal officials suspected of espionage, and coordination of multi-agency responses to national security threats. The agency's first director, Admiral Clayton Westbrook, ran operations from a converted warehouse in Alexandria, Virginia, with a staff of thirty-seven former military intelligence officers.

The 1950s saw JCA's rapid expansion. The agency played an undisclosed role in several high-profile espionage cases, developed advanced surveillance methodologies, and established the framework for domestic counterintelligence that remains in use today. By 1962, JCA operated field offices in forty-three cities and maintained a network of confidential informants across critical industries, government agencies, and academic institutions.

Following the Church Committee investigations of the 1970s, JCA underwent significant reform. Congressional oversight was strengthened, operational authorities were codified under Title 50, and the agency adopted stricter protocols governing domestic surveillance and information sharing. These reforms, while limiting certain activities, ultimately legitimized JCA's role within the intelligence community and secured permanent funding authorization.

The post-9/11 era brought renewed urgency to JCA's mission. The agency absorbed responsibilities for counterterrorism finance investigations, cybersecurity incident response, and protection of critical infrastructure. Today, JCA operates eighty-nine field offices, maintains partnerships with allied intelligence services in thirty-two nations, and employs approximately 8,400 personnel across investigative, analytical, technical, and administrative roles.

Mission Statement

"To protect and defend the United States against threats to national security through strategic intelligence operations, criminal investigations, and counterintelligence activities, while upholding the Constitution and serving the American people with integrity, accountability, and unwavering commitment to justice."

Jurisdiction & Authority

The Joseph Coolidge Agency operates under specific statutory authorities granted by Congress and executive orders issued by the President. Primary jurisdiction includes:

  • Counterintelligence: Investigation of espionage, foreign intelligence operations, and influence campaigns targeting U.S. government personnel, contractors, and critical infrastructure.
  • Federal Crimes: Enforcement authority for violations including public corruption, interstate organized crime, terrorism financing, and crimes committed on federal property or against federal officials.
  • Cybersecurity: Response to cyber incidents affecting federal networks, election systems, financial infrastructure, and designated critical sectors including energy, telecommunications, and transportation.
  • Intelligence Collection: Authorized collection of foreign intelligence within the United States, subject to Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) requirements and oversight by the FISA Court.
  • Protective Operations: Security for designated federal officials, visiting foreign dignitaries, and witnesses in cases of national significance.

JCA operates in coordination with the Department of Justice, reporting to the Attorney General on criminal matters while maintaining a direct reporting relationship with the National Security Council on intelligence operations. The agency is subject to oversight by the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence, the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence, and the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board.
